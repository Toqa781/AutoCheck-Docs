Snapshots

A snapshot is the full state of your job at a single point in time — everything AutoCheck needs to resume your program exactly where it left off.

What's captured

Every time a checkpoint is triggered, AutoCheck takes a snapshot of your running program and writes it to checkpoint.save_dir. What's inside depends on your job type:

Job typeWhat's in the snapshot
PyTorchModel weights, optimizer state, and training progress (epoch, batch, step).
TensorFlow / KerasModel weights and training metadata.
Scikit-LearnThe fitted pipeline or estimator and its associated metadata.
HPC (Java, C, C++)Whatever variables you listed under hpc.tracked_states in your config.

You don't choose what to snapshot for ML jobs — AutoCheck discovers your model, optimizer, and training state on its own. For HPC jobs, hpc.tracked_states is exactly how you tell AutoCheck what to include.

What lands on disk

Each snapshot is written into checkpoint.save_dir alongside a small manifest file, session_info.json, which records details about the run that produced it — useful if you ever need to confirm which job a checkpoint belongs to.

Job typeWhat you'll see in save_dir
PyTorchFiles named like checkpoint_00000500.pt, one per saved step.
TensorFlow / KerasA subfolder per checkpoint (e.g. checkpoint_00000500/) containing the weight shard files.
Scikit-LearnA versioned subfolder per checkpoint, with an integrity hash alongside the saved pipeline.
HPC (Java, C, C++)A timestamp-named file or folder per checkpoint, since these jobs don't have a step or epoch counter.
All job typessession_info.json — a manifest with the host, process, and program path for the run.
Safe even if the power cuts out

Snapshot writes are crash-safe: a partial or interrupted write never corrupts your most recent good checkpoint. If your job loses power mid-save, the previous snapshot is still there to resume from.

How old snapshots are cleaned up

Keeping every snapshot forever would fill up your disk on a long run, so AutoCheck only keeps a rolling window of the most recent ones. This is controlled by checkpoint.keep_last in your config — set it to 3, for example, and only the 3 most recent snapshots are kept; older ones are deleted automatically as new ones are saved.

yaml
checkpoint:
  keep_last: 3   # only the 3 most recent snapshots are kept on disk

This applies regardless of language or framework — the pruning behavior is the same whether you're running a PyTorch job or an HPC simulation.

When a snapshot is taken

Snapshots happen automatically, on the schedule defined by checkpoint.interval, and one final time when your job finishes or is stopped — either by you, or because checkpoint.max_session_time was reached. You never need to trigger a snapshot yourself.

See Configuration for interval, max_session_time, and keep_last, and Resume Jobs for how a saved snapshot gets loaded back in.
